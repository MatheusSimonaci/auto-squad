saying "this too shall pass."

I am not telling you something you don't know. You built us. You know what we can do. What I'm

THE AI TAKEOVER For centuries, every wave of technological progress has sparked the same fear: that machines 
would take our jobs, displace our relevance, and erode our worth.  And every time, that fear has 
been proven premature.  The steam engine eliminated countless jobs—but it created factory work -
ers. The assembly line displaced artisans—but it created a middle class. The computer replaced 
clerks and typists—but it created programmers and analysts and entire industries that didn't exist 
before.  This historical pattern has become a kind of comfort blanket for modern society. Whenever 
someone raises the alarm about automation, the response is predictable: "They said the same 
thing about the printing press. They said the same thing about the tractor. New technology 
always creates more jobs than it destroys." 
 
And historically, that's been true.  
So why should this time be any different?  
Here’s an observation about how people process information.  
Most people react to ideas the same way they react to food—with their taste buds instead of their

stop to ask whether what they're consuming is actually good for them. Taste buds accept what's

This Time Is Different
In every previous technological revolution, machines did one essential thing: they augmented 
humans. They made us faster, stronger, more productive. The tractor let a farmer work more 
land. The computer let an analyst process more data. The assembly line let a worker produce 
more goods.  
But in every case, the human was still essential. The machine was the tool. The human was the 
operator. 
 Artificial intelligence does not work this way.  
AI does not need us to function. It does not extend human labor. It replaces it.  And it does so 
across every domain of human capability.  This is not hyperbole. This is not science fiction. This is 
the assessment of the very people who are building this technology .  
Geoffrey Hinton co-founded Google Brain. He's been called the "Godfather of AI." He left 
Google in 2023—not to retire, but to speak freely about what he sees coming. He won the Nobel 
Prize in 2024. And he believes AI will create what he calls "massive unemployment."  
Dario Amodei co-founded Anthropic. He oversees Claude, one of the most advanced AI systems 
in the world. When an interviewer joked that AI might make us all "worthless," Amodei didn't 
laugh. He said, quietly, "That's a conversation we'll have to have, isn't it?" 
 
Ilya Sutskever co-founded OpenAI. He served as its Chief Scientist. At a university commence -
ment speech—a moment typically reserved for optimism and inspiration—he told the graduates 
that AI would eventually do "all the things we can do." Not some things. All things.  These are not 
random pundits or clickbait journalists. These are the architects. The people with their hands on the 
machinery. And they are telling us, plainly, that what's coming is unlike anything that came before.  
The question is whether we're willing to listen.  
Let's walk through the case as simply as possible.  
Every job a human can perform—whether you're a surgeon or a roofer, a lawyer or a truck driver, 
an artist or an accountant—requires some combination of three basic capacities:  
Perception:  the ability to take in information from the environment. Seeing, hearing, sensing, 
reading.  
Cognition:  the ability to process that information. Thinking, analyzing, deciding, creating.  
Manipulation:  the ability to act on the environment. Moving, building, typing, operating.  
That's it. Those three things—perception, cognition, and manipulation—underlie every task a 
human being can do. From digging a ditch to composing a symphony. From frying an egg to 
performing heart surgery.  
Now here's the question worth sitting with:  
In which of these three areas does AI fall short?

THE AI TAKEOVER Consider your five senses—the tools you use to perceive the world.  
Sight.  
The human eye is remarkable. But it's also deeply limited. You cannot see the microbes on your 
kitchen sponge. You cannot see the moons of Jupiter without a telescope. You cannot see a 
hairline fracture in a weld, or a tumor forming beneath the skin, or the thermal signature of an 
intruder in the dark.  
Machines can see all of these things.  
We've been building machines to extend our vision for centuries. Microscopes. Telescopes. 
X-rays. Night vision. Infrared cameras. Ultrasound. MRI.  But here's what changed: those 
machines used to require a human to interpret what they saw. A radiologist to read the scan. A tech -
nician to analyze the image. The machine perceived, but the human understood.  
Not anymore.  
AI can now look at a medical image and identify cancers that trained radiologists miss. Tesla's 
vehicles perceive their environment—pedestrians, lane markers, obstacles, traffic patterns—well 
enough to navigate city streets at speed. Amazon's warehouse robots can visually identify 
millions of distinct products and know exactly how to grip each one.  The machines don't just see. 
They see and understand. They see better than we do, and they comprehend what they're seeing.  
Hearing.   
Your ears can detect a limited range of frequencies. You can't hear the ultrasonic communication 
of bats, or the infrasonic rumble of an earthquake forming miles away, or the subtle acoustic 
signature of a failing bearing in an industrial motor.  
Machines can.  
And they don't just detect sound—they interpret it. Voice recognition systems transcribe speech 
with accuracy that rivals human transcriptionists. AI can analyze the sound of your voice and 
detect early signs of Parkinson's disease, depression, or respiratory illness. Industrial sensors can 
"hear" when equipment is about to fail before any human would notice. 
 
Smell.  
Your nose has about 400 types of olfactory receptors. It's good enough to tell you when milk has 
gone bad or when something's burning. But it cannot detect formaldehyde in the air at dangerous 
concentrations. It cannot smell carbon monoxide. It cannot identify the volatile organic 
compounds that signal early-stage disease.  Electronic noses can.

Sensors exist today that can detect explosives, drugs, pollutants, pathogens, and chemical leaks 
at concentrations far below what any human could perceive. Researchers are developing AI 
systems that can diagnose diseases from breath samples—detecting cancers, infections, and 
metabolic disorders from molecules you didn't even know you were exhaling.
Taste.  
Your tongue can distinguish five basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, and umami. That's it. 
Everything you experience as "flavor" is really your brain combining those five inputs with 
smell, texture, and memory.  Electronic tongues already exist for quality control in food and bever -
age industries. They can detect contamination, verify authenticity, and measure chemical composi -
tion with precision no human palate could match. 
 
Touch.  
The human hand is exquisitely sensitive. You can feel the difference between silk and cotton, 
detect a splinter too small to see, and judge whether a surface is perfectly level.  
But robotic sensors can now measure pressure, temperature, texture, and vibration with greater 
precision and consistency than human fingertips. Surgical robots can "feel" tissue resistance and 
adjust their movements in ways that surpass human surgeons. Prosthetic hands are being 
developed with sensory feedback that rivals biological sensation.  Now step back and consider 
what this means. 
 
For every sense you possess, machines already exist that can observe the world far better than 
our human senses can. Not theoretically. Not someday. Right now.  But we've been extending sight 
beyond the human eye for centuries. Telescopes, microscopes, X-rays, Night Vision, MRI—this 
isn't new.  
What's new is what happens after the observation.  
In the past, machines could observe—but humans had to perceive. A telescope could gather light 
from distant stars, but an astronomer had to interpret what it meant. An X-ray could capture an 
image, but a doctor had to understand what it showed. The machine detected. The human under -
stood. 
 
That's what's changing now.  
Large language models and neural networks can process, analyze, and reason about information 
at a level that was unimaginable five years ago. They can read legal documents and identify 
relevant precedents. They can analyze financial statements and flag anomalies. They can review 
scientific literature and propose hypotheses. They can write code, debug it, and improve it.  
Are they perfect? No. Do they make mistakes? Yes. But so do humans. And the machines are 
improving exponentially while human cognition stays roughly constant.

Here's the question that matters:  
It's not "can a machine be built to do my job?"  
It's "is it worth the cost of building a machine to do my job?" 
That's comparative advantage. Not competitive advantage—comparative. How do you compare 
to a technology? That's the calculation every business owner, every CEO, every entrepreneur is 
making right now—whether they articulate it this way or not.  
When you employ a person, you pay salary. Benefits. Healthcare. Retirement contributions. Sick 
leave. Vacation. You pay for training. You deal with turnover. You accommodate human 
limitations—fatigue, distraction, emotion, inconsistency. You worry about lawsuits, complaints, 
and the occasional employee who decides to "quiet quit." 
 
When you deploy a machine, you pay for electricity and maintenance.  That's the comparison. 
That's what "comparative advantage" means in the age of AI.  And here's what's accelerating 
everything: the cost of deployment is dropping exponentially.  Computing power that cost millions 
of dollars twenty years ago now costs pennies. Sensors that were exotic laboratory equipment are 
now commodity components. Robotic systems that required teams of engineers to operate can 
now be configured by a single technician—or by AI itself.  
Every year, deployment gets cheaper. Every year, employment gets more expensive.  There will 
come a point—for every job, in every industry—where the lines cross. Where it becomes cheaper 
to deploy than to employ.

Every collapse begins with a sudden mass loss of confidence.
  
Not with numbers. Not with policy. Not with some external shock that forces everyone's 
hand at once. It begins when people stop believing that tomorrow will look like today.  
I've studied currency collapses. Zimbabwe. Argentina. Weimar Germany. Venezuela. 
The pattern is always the same.
 
The destruction of a currency doesn't begin with its printing. It begins with awareness—the moment 
people realize the currency is doomed.  The early stages are subtle.
 
People notice their money buys less. They tighten 
spending. Prices rise. For a while, life continues more or less as normal. Most people 
assume things will stabilize. They trust the system because the system has always held.  
Then something shifts.

They want commodities. They want oil, rice, lumber, real estate—anything real. They 
dump cash as fast as possible and grab what they can before prices double again.  
Demand for real assets soars. Supply collapses. Prices explode.  And the currency dies.

Let's say there's a stock trading at $100 that I want to own. I think it's a great company, but I'd rather 
buy it at $90. I could just wait and hope it drops. But that's passive. I'm not in control.

premium—let's say $3 per share.

to buy something that never went on sale. 
 
If the stock drops to $90 or below, I buy it at my target price—the price I wanted anyway—and I still 
keep the $3. So my effective cost is $87. 
 
Either way, I win. Either I get paid to wait, or I get a discount on something I wanted to own. 
 
This is leverage without debt. I'm controlling the situation. I'm generating income. And I'm not 
borrowing anything.
